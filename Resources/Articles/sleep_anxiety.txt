If your mind starts racing the moment you lie down, you are not alone. Sleep anxiety — the cycle of anxiety making sleep harder and lack of sleep making anxiety worse — is one of the most common sleep struggles people face.

Your brain is designed to stay alert when it perceives threat. When you are anxious, it is harder to move into the relaxed state that sleep requires. The harder you try to force sleep, the more frustrated and alert you become — which only makes things worse.

Breaking the cycle starts with reducing the pressure around sleep itself.

Strategies that help:

Stop clock-watching. Checking the time repeatedly activates your brain's problem-solving mode and increases anxiety. Turn your clock or phone face-down.

Get out of bed if you cannot sleep. Lying awake for extended periods trains your brain to associate your bed with wakefulness. If you are awake for more than 20–30 minutes, get up and do something calm and quiet in low light until you feel sleepy.

Practice diaphragmatic breathing. Slow, deep breaths from your belly activate the parasympathetic nervous system and shift your body away from the stress response. Try breathing in for 4 counts, holding for 4, and out for 6–8.

Use progressive muscle relaxation. Systematically tensing and releasing muscle groups from your feet upward helps reduce physical tension that anxiety creates.

Write it down. If racing thoughts are the problem, keeping a notepad by your bed to offload worries before sleep can genuinely help.

Addressing the root causes of anxiety — through therapy, lifestyle changes, or both — is the most effective long-term approach. But these tools can help you manage sleep in the meantime.
