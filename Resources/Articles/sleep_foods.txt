What you eat has a meaningful impact on how well you sleep. Growing evidence from clinical trials and institutional research — including studies from Columbia University, the University of Chicago, and Johns Hopkins Medicine — supports the role of specific foods and dietary patterns in improving sleep quality, duration, and how quickly you fall asleep.
 
Foods with strong research backing:
 
Tart cherries are one of the few natural food sources of melatonin. Research cited by Columbia University found that drinking tart cherry juice twice daily for two weeks significantly reduced insomnia severity and night waking in adults with chronic insomnia. Kiwifruit has similarly strong evidence — a study in the Asia Pacific Journal of Clinical Nutrition found that eating two kiwis an hour before bed for four weeks increased total sleep time and helped people fall asleep faster. Fatty fish such as salmon and mackerel are high in omega-3 fatty acids and vitamin D, both identified as sleep-supportive nutrients, and are recommended by the University of Michigan School of Public Health. Warm milk and dairy products contain tryptophan and calcium, which support melatonin production — some of the earliest clinical sleep research involved milk-based drinks. Chamomile tea is backed by studies in Phytotherapy Research showing it significantly improves sleep quality and reduces generalised anxiety, likely through its effect on GABA receptors in the brain. Magnesium-rich foods — pumpkin seeds, bananas, and walnuts — help regulate melatonin and calm the nervous system by binding to the same receptors targeted by some sleep medications.
 
Beyond individual foods, overall dietary patterns matter. A study funded by the National Institutes of Health and led by the University of Chicago and Columbia University found that people who ate more fruits, vegetables, and whole grains during the day had measurably deeper, less fragmented sleep that same night — objectively measured, not self-reported. Meeting the CDC recommendation of five cups of fruit and vegetables per day was estimated to improve sleep quality by around 16 percent. The Mediterranean diet, consistently associated with better sleep in multiple studies, reflects this principle: it is not one food but a pattern of eating that is high in plants, healthy fats, and fibre, and low in processed food and added sugar.
 
What disrupts sleep is equally worth knowing. High-fat meals, refined sugar, spicy food close to bedtime, and alcohol — even though alcohol initially helps you fall asleep — are all associated with more fragmented, lower quality sleep. Late-night eating in general is linked to poorer sleep outcomes.
 
No single food will fix poor sleep. But consistently eating in a way that is high in fibre, fruits, vegetables, and anti-inflammatory foods is one of the most accessible and well-evidenced steps you can take to support it.
