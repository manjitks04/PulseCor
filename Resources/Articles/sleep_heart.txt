Sleep and heart health are more connected than most people realise. During sleep, your heart rate slows, your blood pressure drops, and your cardiovascular system gets a chance to recover and repair.

When you consistently get too little sleep — less than 7 hours per night — your body compensates by keeping stress hormones elevated. Over time, this raises blood pressure, increases inflammation, and puts extra strain on your heart and blood vessels.

Research has linked poor sleep to a higher risk of high blood pressure, heart disease and heart attacks, stroke, irregular heart rhythms including atrial fibrillation, and type 2 diabetes, which significantly increases cardiovascular risk.

The connection works both ways. Poor sleep raises cardiovascular risk, and cardiovascular conditions can significantly disrupt sleep. Sleep apnoea, for example, causes breathing to repeatedly pause during sleep, putting strain on the heart each time and is closely linked to high blood pressure.

Signs your sleep may be affecting your heart:

Waking up with headaches or a racing heart. Feeling exhausted after a full night's sleep. High blood pressure readings, especially in the morning. Loud snoring or being told you stop breathing in your sleep.

The good news is that improving sleep has a measurable positive effect on heart health. Even adding one hour of sleep per night has been shown to reduce cardiovascular risk in people who consistently sleep fewer than 6 hours.

Prioritising sleep is not just about energy. For your heart, consistent quality sleep is one of the most impactful daily habits you can build.
