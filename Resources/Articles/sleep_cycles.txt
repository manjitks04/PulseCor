Your sleep is made up of repeating 90-minute cycles, each containing different stages: light sleep, deep sleep, and REM (rapid eye movement) sleep.

Deep sleep is your body's repair mode. During this stage, tissue grows and repairs, your immune system strengthens, and memories consolidate. REM sleep is when your brain processes emotions, clears mental clutter, and supports creativity.

Most adults need 4–6 complete cycles per night — that's roughly 7–9 hours of sleep. Waking in the middle of a cycle is why you sometimes feel groggy even after plenty of time in bed.

Timing your alarm to align with the end of a cycle can make a real difference. If you fall asleep at 11pm, setting your alarm for 6:30am — 4.5 cycles plus around 15 minutes to fall asleep — gives your body a better chance of waking at a natural transition point.

Your sleep architecture also shifts across the night. Early cycles have more deep sleep; later cycles have more REM. This is why getting a full night's sleep matters — cutting it short costs you the REM sleep your brain needs most.

How to protect your sleep cycles:

Keep your wake time consistent, even on weekends. Avoid alcohol close to bedtime — it fragments REM sleep. Get morning daylight to anchor your body clock. Avoid caffeine after 2pm, as it has a half-life of 5–7 hours. If you wake in the night, avoid checking the time — it triggers alertness.

Understanding sleep cycles helps explain why sleep quality and timing matter just as much as the number of hours you spend in bed. It is not just about duration — it is about letting your body complete the full process it is designed to run every night.
