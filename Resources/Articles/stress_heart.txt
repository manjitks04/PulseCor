Stress and your heart are deeply connected. While short-term stress is a normal part of life and your body is well-equipped to handle it, chronic stress — the ongoing, low-grade kind that never fully resolves — takes a significant toll on cardiovascular health.

When you are stressed, your body releases cortisol and adrenaline. These hormones trigger the fight-or-flight response: your heart beats faster, your blood vessels narrow, and your blood pressure rises. In the short term, this response is protective. When it is constantly activated, it becomes harmful.

How chronic stress affects your heart:

Persistently elevated blood pressure strains artery walls and accelerates the build-up of fatty plaques. Chronic inflammation — partly driven by cortisol — damages blood vessels over time. Stress triggers unhealthy coping behaviours such as overeating, smoking, excessive alcohol use, and physical inactivity — all cardiovascular risk factors. Poor sleep driven by stress further raises blood pressure and inflammation markers. Stress can trigger irregular heartbeats and has been linked to sudden cardiac events.

Protecting your heart from stress:

Physical activity is one of the most effective stress reducers — it burns off stress hormones and releases endorphins. Breathing exercises activate the parasympathetic nervous system, directly lowering heart rate and blood pressure. Social connection buffers the cardiovascular effects of stress — people with strong social support have significantly lower heart disease risk. Sleep is non-negotiable, as adequate sleep reduces cortisol and allows your cardiovascular system to recover. Mindfulness and meditation have demonstrated measurable reductions in blood pressure in clinical studies.

You do not need to eliminate stress. What matters is how quickly you recover from it, and how consistently you invest in the habits that help you do so.
