Knowing when to seek help — and acting on it — is one of the most important things you can do for your heart health. Many heart events are preceded by warning signs that people dismiss or delay acting on.

Signs that require immediate medical attention — call 999:

Chest pain or tightness, particularly if it feels like pressure, squeezing, or heaviness. It may radiate to your left arm, neck, jaw, or back. Sudden shortness of breath at rest. Sudden dizziness, lightheadedness, or loss of consciousness. Sudden severe headache with no obvious cause, which can indicate stroke. Face drooping, arm weakness, or speech difficulty — these are the classic signs of stroke. Use the FAST acronym: Face, Arms, Speech, Time. Heart palpitations combined with chest pain, dizziness, or fainting.

Signs worth discussing with your GP — non-emergency but important:

Persistent fatigue or reduced exercise tolerance that is new or unexplained. Swollen ankles or feet, especially if new. Frequent palpitations or awareness of an irregular heartbeat. Shortness of breath during mild activity that you could previously do without difficulty. Occasional dizziness or lightheadedness, particularly when standing up.

Women may experience different or more subtle heart attack symptoms than the classic crushing chest pain — including nausea, jaw pain, upper back pain, and extreme fatigue. These symptoms are often dismissed or attributed to something else.

Trust your body. If something feels significantly wrong — especially if it came on suddenly — do not wait and see. The window for effective treatment in heart attacks and strokes is narrow. Acting quickly saves lives.
