Blue light is a wavelength of light that has a particularly strong effect on your brain's sleep-wake system. While it comes from the sun and helps you feel awake and alert during the day, artificial blue light from screens in the evening can significantly delay your sleep.

Your brain produces melatonin — the hormone that signals it is time to sleep — in response to darkness. Blue light suppresses melatonin production directly, which can push back your natural sleep window by 1–3 hours depending on your exposure.

Common sources of evening blue light include smartphones, tablets, and laptops, LED televisions, energy-efficient LED lighting, and fluorescent lighting.

Beyond melatonin suppression, screen use before bed keeps your brain cognitively stimulated — especially social media, news, and anything that triggers emotional responses. Your brain is not just being exposed to light; it is being kept mentally active when it should be winding down.

What you can do:

Use Night Mode or Warm Mode on your devices — most smartphones have a setting to reduce blue light emission after sunset. Switch to warmer, dimmer lighting in the evening — lamps rather than overhead lights. Try blue light blocking glasses if you regularly use screens in the evening. Set a no-screens rule 30–60 minutes before bed, or gradually build towards this. Use calmer apps in the evening — reading rather than social media — since the content matters as much as the screen itself.

The goal is not to eliminate all screens but to reduce your exposure during the hours when your brain is preparing for sleep. Even small reductions in evening blue light exposure can make falling asleep faster and more natural.
