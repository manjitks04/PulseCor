Your heart rate is one of the simplest and most informative measures of your cardiovascular health. Understanding what your numbers mean — and what affects them — can help you tune in to your body in a meaningful way.

Resting heart rate is the number of times your heart beats per minute when you are at complete rest. A healthy range for adults is 60–100 beats per minute, though many fit and active people sit between 40 and 60 — a sign of a strong, efficient heart that does not need to work as hard to maintain circulation.

To measure your resting heart rate accurately, take it first thing in the morning before getting out of bed. Count your heartbeats for 60 seconds, or for 30 seconds and multiply by two. Use your index and middle fingers on the inside of your wrist or the side of your neck — avoid using your thumb, which has its own pulse. A fitness tracker can give you an average over time, which is more reliable than a single reading.

What your resting heart rate can tell you:

A consistently high resting heart rate above 80–90 can indicate stress, poor fitness, dehydration, illness, or underlying cardiovascular issues worth investigating. A gradually decreasing resting heart rate over weeks of exercise is a sign your cardiovascular fitness is improving. A sudden unexplained spike — especially if you feel unwell — is worth noting and discussing with your GP.

Heart rate variability is another emerging measure — the variation in time between beats. A higher variability is associated with better recovery, resilience to stress, and cardiovascular health. Many wearables now track this automatically.

Knowing your baseline resting heart rate and tracking it over time gives you a simple, free way to monitor your cardiovascular health from home.
