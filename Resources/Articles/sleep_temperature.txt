Your body temperature plays a more important role in sleep than most people realise. As part of your circadian rhythm, your core body temperature naturally drops in the evening — and this drop is one of the key signals that initiates sleep. A cool sleeping environment supports this process; a warm one works against it.

The ideal bedroom temperature for sleep:

Research consistently suggests that a bedroom temperature between 16°C and 19°C (60°F–66°F) is optimal for most adults. This feels cooler than most people expect, but it supports the natural drop in core temperature that your body needs to fall and stay asleep. Temperatures above 24°C significantly disrupt sleep quality, particularly REM sleep.

How temperature affects sleep stages:

Deep sleep is particularly sensitive to body temperature. Being too warm reduces the amount of slow-wave deep sleep you get, even if you do not consciously wake. REM sleep is also disrupted by heat. Both are the restorative stages your body and brain need most.

Practical ways to optimise sleep temperature:

Keep your bedroom cooler than the rest of your home at night. Use a fan or crack a window if necessary. Take a warm shower or bath about an hour before bed — this sounds counterintuitive, but it warms the skin, causing heat to dissipate from the body and triggering a helpful drop in core temperature. Choose breathable bedding — natural materials like cotton and linen regulate temperature better than synthetic fabrics. Keep your feet warm if the rest of your body is cool — warming your feet helps dilate blood vessels and speeds up the process of core cooling. Avoid heavy exercise within two hours of bed, as it raises core temperature.

Small changes to your sleep environment temperature can have a noticeable effect on how quickly you fall asleep and how deeply you rest through the night.
