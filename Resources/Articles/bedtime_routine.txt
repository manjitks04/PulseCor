A consistent bedtime routine signals your brain that sleep is coming — and your brain responds faster than you might expect.

Your body runs on a 24-hour internal clock called the circadian rhythm. When you repeat the same pre-sleep behaviours each night, your brain begins to associate those actions with sleep, releasing melatonin earlier and helping you fall asleep faster with less effort.

Building a routine does not have to be complicated. Even a 20–30 minute wind-down can make a meaningful difference.

What to include:

Dim the lights at least an hour before bed — bright overhead lights suppress melatonin production. Set your phone to Do Not Disturb and step away from screens where possible. Try a warm shower or bath — the drop in your body temperature afterwards is a natural sleep trigger. Do something calming: reading, journalling, gentle stretching, or slow breathing exercises. Aim for the same bedtime each night, within 30 minutes.

What to avoid:

Scrolling social media or watching intense content close to bed. Heavy meals in the two hours before sleep. Caffeine after 2pm — its effects can linger for 5–7 hours. Checking work emails or anything that activates problem-solving mode.

The routine itself matters less than its consistency. Your brain learns to associate the sequence of habits with sleep onset. Over time, starting your routine begins to feel naturally calming — almost like flipping a switch.

Start small. Pick two or three habits and repeat them for a week before adding more. Consistency is what makes a routine work, not perfection. Even a simple, imperfect routine repeated nightly beats a complex one that never happens.
